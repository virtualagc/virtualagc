Introduction

This page of the Virtual AGC website is very technical, and not likely to be of interest unless you are very familiar with electronics design.  The goal of this page is to present the complete electrical schematics and mechanical drawings for every major version of the AGC and DSKY units.  We don't yet fully succeed in doing so.  Additionally, there are drawings related to various intermediate versions of the AGC and DSKY which we point out to you as a resource, but let you digest them for yourself if you're interested, rather than trying to do so for you.

"Versions" of the AGC and DSKY? Huh?

Well, like the AGC's software, the AGC's hardware (electrical and mechanical design) evolved over time.  For example, not all AGC electrical schematics are created equal ... you have to know which specific AGC model number you're interested in, since its electrical schematics and mechanical configuration may be different than other AGC models.  There were a lot of different versions of the AGC over time, and not just the major distinction between Block I and Block II AGCs with which you may be familiar.  The same comment goes for DSKY units (and other components as well, though we won't concern ourselves with any of them).  The good news is that the changes were prior to any actual missions, and mostly didn't affect software, so there was much less variation in electrical design from mission to mission than there was for the software design.

The AGC and DSKY part-numbering scheme looked like this:

PARTNUMBER-DASHNUMBER REVISION

For example, Block II AGCs had PARTNUMBERs 2003100, 2003200, and 2003993, with DASHNUMBERs like -011, -021, -031, and so on.  REVISIONs were letter codes, such as A, B, C, etc., or else a dash (-) for "no revision".  For example,

2003200-011A

Given a part number for a particular AGC (or DSKY) model, the part number then relates to a set of sub-assemblies (often represented by electrical schematics or mechanical drawings), many of which are circuit modules, which themselves may have varying versions over time, and have part-numbers like

PARTNUMBER REVISION

For example, the AGC with p/n 2003200-011A contained, among other things, an "oscillator module" which fit into a backplane socket labeled B7, and which itself had a p/n 2005003E ... and naturally, we have drawing 2005003E if you want to see it.

Sadly, in most cases, though with some happy rare exceptions, we don't presently have enough information to actually tell you which versions of every AGC or DSKY flew on which missions anyway.  Oddly, even though we don't know their part numbers, we do have enough information to know which serial numbers of AGC and DSKY units flew on which missions.  That information is given in an Appendix. 

In general, we think that all Block II missions flew with some variant of AGC p/n 2003993-xxx and DSKY p/n 2003994-xxx, but irritatingly we presently have only partial drawing sets for part numbers 2003993 and 2003994!  Whereas we have complete drawing sets for the earlier Block II AGC part numbers (2003100 and 2003200) and DSKY part numbers (2003985 and 2003950).  This would naturally lead one to ask how great the differences were between all these different AGC part numbers?  Well, Mike Stewart has put together a nice, little list of the differences we're aware of; I quote him:

Changes from AGC p/n 2003100 to 2003200:

  • Conversion from aluminum trays (the X on the tray B cover, as depicted in most drawings) to magnesium trays (with the grid on the tray B cover).
  • Addition of dedicated "expander gates" to replace all of the blue-nose gates in the 2003100 design, whose power pins were left disconnected. There was a diode sneak path in the dual NOR gate integrated circuit that manifested in these blue nose gates, that added significant coupling between the two gates in a chip, and added excessive load to some nets. The custom expander gates had the resistor from the power pin to the outputs removed, which achieved the same effect, but the diode sneak path was still there, and it needed to be reverse-biased to keep it from affecting anything. So a new power rail, called "FAP", was added (shorted to +4VDC in both power supplies), that kept constant power applied to all of these gates, even during standby.
  • Addition of wiring to allow the "STBY" key on the DSKY to be used as "PRO". I tested for such wiring in the AGC I was examining, and it wasn't there, so I'm guessing this capability wasn't ported back to any 2003100 computers.
  • Addition of additional diodes in the fixed and erasable memory drivers, which helped with thermal stability.
  • Addition of a resistor/diode feedback path to get rope memory working (these components were hacked across the backplane, in the yellow sleeves in Tray B, for the AGC I was examining).
  • Addition of a capacitor in the erasable memory module that allowed the exact timing of the sense amplifier strobe to be adjusted. As far as I can tell, two extra pins were physically added to the erasable memory module for this, since there are no such spare pins in 2003100 erasable modules.
  • Switching of the potting compound used in the erasable memory module. There was a problem where the original compound allowed two much movement of the cores, which was leading to some of the wires breaking internally. (I may be wrong about the timing of this one; it may have been between 2003200 and 2003993, or maybe even later than that).
  • Addition of a dedicated 26.5 Volt supply for the DSKY to the two power supply modules.
  • Some changed resistor configurations in the alarms module, again for thermal stability.
  • Also somewhere in here, the tuning resistors in the interface modules were changed, to adjust the number of counts at max deflection of the RHC/ACA in the LM from 32 to 42. I'm guessing that was probably between 2003100 and 2003200, but I'm not completely sure.

Changes from AGC p/n 2003200 to 2003993:

  • Removal of the 26.5 Volt supply for the DSKY that had been added in 2003200 (I still don't know what this was supposed to be for).
  • Updates to support the Auxiliary Memory Unit being developed at Raytheon, which would give the AGC access to an external core memory stack and tape module. These updates include wiring to some spare NOR gates (and possibly the population of those NOR gates, if they chips were omitted from the boards), and wiring a new Monitor signal, MAMU (Monitor Auxiliary Memory Unit), to the test connector A52.
  • Addition of a test connector cover that grounded all of the Monitor inputs (this change was backported all the way back to 2003100 AGCs).
  • Rerouting of the SCAFAL wire in the Tray B backplane to eliminate noise coupling into it.
  • Switching to a new diode, or something like that?

In particular, it sounds like the biggest change from AGC p/n 2003200 to 2003993 was the support for the Raytheon Auxiliary Memory Unit.  Over on our LUMINARY page, we actually have a complete AGC program whose purpose is exercising and testing this very Auxiliary Memory Unit.  Nevertheless, the Auxiliary Memory Unit was neither installed nor used on actual missions, so I think you'd be justified in believing that the 2003200 AGC is pretty close to being a 2003993 AGC.

More drawings are available than just those presented specifically on this page.  The full sets of drawings available to us at the present time are these:

  • Folder of JPG scans of drawings preserved by Eldon Hall.  Contains most of the available drawings for AGC p/n 2003993.
  • The "AGC Handbook", contributed by Don Eyles.  Contains most of the drawings for AGC/DSKY part numbers other than AGC p/n 2003993.  Contains many other drawings than just the electrical schematics, including top-level indices, mechanical drawings, wiring diagrams, assembly drawings, and even software lists.  The AGC Handbook scans are available in two forms:
    • Reduced-size (170MB), reduced-quality PDF hosted locally here at the Virtual AGC website.
    • Full-quality scans, hosted on our Virtual AGC project page at the Internet Archive.  The drawings can be individually viewed in full-quality form online there, or else you can download the entire set of drawings (4.2GB) in JPEG2000 format.
Often, some revision of a drawing which is not part of one of the AGC/DSKY assemblies listed on this page, but nevertheless appears in the AGC Handbook, is more legible than the corresponding revision we are listing here.  So it's often useful to look at those other revisions of the drawings in additions to the ones we are listing here on this page.  In the handful of cases in which the same drawing appears in both Eldon's and Don's set, the one in Eldon's set will be more legible.

Besides these primary sources, there are additional (sometimes overlapping) portions of electrical schematics available in AC Electronics manual ND-1021042 that haven't (or haven't yet) been integrated into this particular framework, but which can be used to fill in some gaps in the official schematics of AGC p/n 2003993 and DSKY p/n 2003994 that we do have from the sources mentioned above.  Other such alternate or partial AGC/DSKY schematics may be available in the "AGC electrical schematics" section of our document-library page.

Additional information for used for cross-referencing the various drawings to specific AGC or DSKY part numbers and/or serial numbers came from a couple of sources:
  • Section 3.7 of the AC Electronics Final Report on Contract NAS9-497 and tables 7-IV and 7-VI of the Instrumentation Labs Final Report on Contracts NAS9-153 and NAS9-4065 can be used to relate many AGC and DSKY serial numbers to the specific missions (or in some cases, other purposes) for which they were used.  In some cases, there is a discrepancy between these two documents (e.g., which DSKYs were assigned to LM-10), in which case the latter document was chosen to be authoritative.
  • Table 8-II in AC Electronics LM PGNCS Manual ND-1021042 (volume 2) and Table 3-IE in volume 1 can be used to obtain drawing numbers of electrical schematics corresponding to the different AGC and DSKY assembly part numbers.

Appendix: Evolution of AGC/DSKY Part Numbers

The way different part numbers of AGCs or DSKYs are related to one another is that you start with a given assembly with a certain part number (such as Block II AGC with p/n 2003100-011), then you apply an Engineering Change Proposal, or ECP for short, such as ECP 322 (described as "computer wiring changes"), with the result that you now have a different assembly p/n 2003100-021. 

Evolution of Block II AGC Part Numbers

The list below shows how all known Block II AGC part numbers are interrelated according to the principle just mentioned.  Many of these changes clearly are not electrical in nature, or else refer to intermediate or test versions of the AGC, so to simplify it a little bit I've highlighted in red the path (I think) from the earliest Block II AGC to the AGC p/n(s) used in flown missions.  (Sometimes there are multiple paths to get to the same part number, if the ECPs are applied in different orders.  For example, p/n 2003993-061 + ECP 815 = p/n 2003993-071 + ECP 719 = p/n 2003993-111.  I've only bothered to show one such path below in the few cases where that happens.)
  • p/n 2003100-011
    • p/n 2003100-021 p/n 2003100-011 + ECP 322 ("computer wiring changes")
      • p/n 2003100-081 p/n 2003100-021 + ECP 474 ("manufacture test connector jumpers to ground certain gate inputs")
      • p/n 2003100-031, -041, -051 p/n 2003100-021 + ECP 301 ("thermal instrumentation")
        • p/n 2003100-061 p/n 2003100-031 + ECP 474 ("manufacture test connector jumpers to ground certain gate inputs")
        • p/n 2003100-071 p/n 2003100-041 + ECP 474 ("manufacture test connector jumpers to ground certain gate inputs")
        • p/n 2003100-081 p/n 2003100-051 + ECP 474 ("manufacture test connector jumpers to ground certain gate inputs")
      • p/n 2003200-011 p/n 2003100-021 + ECP 176 ("computer module vibration") + ECP 226 ("aluminum to magnesium conversion of AGC trays") + ECP 254 ("computer multilayer board layout") + ECP 257 ("redesign of rope and erasable drivers") + ECP 258 ("redesign of power supply module") + ECP 259 ("redesign of erasable memory") + ECP 324 ("sense amplifier threshold voltage stability change") + ECP 351 ("alarm module temperature stabilization of warning integrator and improve oscillator fail alarm") + ECP 368 ("improved power supply module relays") + ECP 402 ("clear circuit driver modification") + ECP 403 ("strobe adjustment") + ECP 470 ("random workmanship vibration")
