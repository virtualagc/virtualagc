Introduction

On this page of the Virtual AGC website, the goal is to present the complete electrical schematics and mechanical drawings for every major version of the AGC and DSKY units.  We don't yet fully succeed in doing so.  Additionally, there are drawings related to various intermediate versions of the AGC and DSKY which we point out to you as a resource, but let you digest them for yourself if you're interested, rather than trying to do so for you.

Versions of the AGC and DSKY? Huh?

Well, like the AGC's software, the AGC's hardware (electrical and mechanical design) evolved over time.  For example, not all AGC electrical schematics are created equal ... you have to know which specific AGC model number you're interested in, since its electrical schematics and mechanical configuration may be different than other AGC models.  There were a lot of different versions of the AGC over time, and not just the major distinction between Block I and Block II AGCs with which you may be familiar.  The same comment goes for DSKY units (and other components as well, though we won't concern ourselves with any of them).  The good news is that the changes were prior to any actual missions, and mostly didn't affect software, so there was much less variation in electrical design from mission to mission than there was for the software design.

The AGC and DSKY part-numbering scheme looked like this:

PARTNUMBER-DASHNUMBER REVISION

For example, Block II AGCs had PARTNUMBERs 2003100, 2003200, and 2003993, with DASHNUMBERs like -011, -021, -031, and so on.  REVISIONs were letter codes, such as A, B, C, etc., or else a dash (-) for "no revision".  For example,

2003200-011A

Given a part number for a particular AGC (or DSKY) model, the part number then relates to a set of sub-assemblies (often represented by electrical schematics or mechanical drawings), many of which are circuit modules, which themselves may have varying versions over time, and have part-numbers like

PARTNUMBER REVISION

For example, the AGC with p/n 2003200-011A contained, among other things, an "oscillator module" which fit into a backplane socket labeled B7, and which itself had a p/n 2005003E ... and naturally, we have drawing 2005003E if you want to see it.

But as I mentioned, to simplify the presentation a little, we concentrate here on just a few selected versions of the AGC and DSKY, namely those listed in the Table of Contents.  Sadly, in most cases, though with some happy rare exceptions, we don't presently have enough information to actually tell you which versions of every AGC or DSKY flew on which missions anyway.  Oddly, even though we don't know their part numbers, we do have enough information to know which serial numbers of AGC and DSKY units flew on which missions.  That information is given in an Appendix.  In general, though, we think that all Block II missions flew with some variant of AGC p/n 2003993-xxx and DSKY p/n 2003994-xxx.

Except where otherwise stated below, all drawings for AGC p/n 2003993-xxx were contributed by Eldon Hall to klabs.org, and all drawings for other AGC/DSKY part numbers come from the so-called AGC Handbook, contributed to Virtual AGC by Don Eyles.  The AGC Handbook is very significant, in that it is the source document which provides all of the assembly information as well as which drawings are associated with which AGC/DSKY versions, and which provides those drawings (in numerous revisions) as well.  Often, some revision of a drawing which is not part of one of the AGC/DSKY assemblies listed on this page is more legible than the corresponding revision we are listing here, so it's often useful to look at those other revisions of the drawings in additions to the ones we are listing here.  The full scan of the AGC Handbook can be seen on our Internet Archive site.  We haven't followed our typical process of providing a "reduced quality" version of the AGC Handbook on our document-library page, because it doesn't seem like reducing the quality of these drawings is that great an idea, so our Internet Archive page is the only source for this data at present.  If you want to download it from there it will be quite large.

There are additional (sometimes overlapping) electrical or mechanical drawings available in AC Electronics guidance-system manuals which haven't (or haven't yet) been integrated into this particular framework, but which can be used to fill in some gaps in the official schematics we do have from the sources mentioned above.  Such information can be seen in the "AGC electrical schematics" section of our document-library page.

Additional information for used for cross-referencing the various drawings to specific AGC or DSKY part numbers and/or serial numbers came from a couple of sources:
  • Section 3.7 of the AC Electronics Final Report on Contract NAS9-497 and tables 7-IV and 7-VI of the Instrumentation Labs Final Report on Contracts NAS9-153 and NAS9-4065 can be used to relate many AGC and DSKY serial numbers to the specific missions (or in some cases, other purposes) for which they were used.  In some cases, there is a discrepancy between these two documents (e.g., which DSKYs were assigned to LM-10), in which case the latter document was chosen to be authoritative.
  • Table 8-II in AC Electronics LM PGNCS Manual ND-1021042 (volume 2) and Table 3-IE in volume 1 can be used to obtain drawing numbers of electrical schematics corresponding to the different AGC and DSKY assembly part numbers.

Appendix: Evolution of AGC/DSKY Part Numbers

The way different part numbers of AGCs or DSKYs are related to one another is that you start with a given assembly with a certain part number (such as Block II AGC with p/n 2003100-011), then you apply an Engineering Change Proposal, or ECP for short, such as ECP 322 (described as "computer wiring changes"), with the result that you now have a different assembly p/n 2003100-021. 

Evolution of Block II AGC Part Numbers

The list below shows how all known Block II AGC part numbers are interrelated according to the principle just mentioned.  Many of these changes clearly are not electrical in nature, or else refer to intermediate or test versions of the AGC, so to simplify it a little bit I've highlighted in red the path (I think) from the earliest Block II AGC to the AGC p/n(s) used in flown missions.  (Sometimes there are multiple paths to get to the same part number, if the ECPs are applied in different orders.  For example, p/n 2003993-061 + ECP 815 = p/n 2003993-071 + ECP 719 = p/n 2003993-111.  I've only bothered to show one such path below in the few cases where that happens.)

      • p/n 2003100-031, -041, -051 p/n 2003100-021 + ECP 301 ("thermal instrumentation")
        • p/n 2003100-061 p/n 2003100-031 + ECP 474 ("manufacture test connector jumpers to ground certain gate inputs")
